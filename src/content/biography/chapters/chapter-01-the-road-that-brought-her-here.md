# Chapter 1 — The Road That Brought Her Here

Kelly grew up in Selmer, Tennessee, in the steady company of extended family—cousins and kin close enough that childhood felt less like a small circle and more like a crowd that knew her name. Her people were hardworking and middle class in the plain sense: not flashy, held together by steady labor and careful choices. Faith sat at the center of that life, not as decoration for visitors but as something her grandparents carried with particular strength; the rest of the family drew in close, unhurried, sharing the same rhythms. What lived in that household was faith as habit—the kind you practice in ordinary rooms on ordinary days, separate from anything that might later harden into performance on a public stage.

Her mother began with the hearth. From childhood she had Kelly stand there and deliver speeches to the family, turning an ordinary evening into the first quiet theater she knew. From that cushion of kin she stepped into 4-H public speaking, where words had to stand without the forgiving warmth of home. Then her father’s work widened the lens. From fifth grade on he served as State Director for Modern Woodmen of America, and through that world she heard motivational voices like Zig Ziglar—speakers who believed a life could turn on language well delivered. She began to dream of the wider circuit herself: stage, microphone, faces lifted toward a story told well. None of it promised a destination; it only filled her with models of what trained breath and nerve could do. Years later, other doors would open—Toastmasters, sales training, the corporate classrooms of Alltel and Verizon—but those belong farther down the road. Here she is still the girl placed where she was placed, learning greatness because someone believed she could.

A job offer made Arkansas possible—an opening before it was home. Before her parents would uproot deep Tennessee roots, they took Kelly and her little brother Ben across the state they might someday claim, so Arkansas would be ground under their feet and not a rumor passed down by adults. Hot Springs, the old Dogpatch grounds, Mountain View, state parks, roadside attractions—and scenic Highway 7 stitching the hills. The family fell in love with Arkansas that way: not because one landmark answered everything, but because the sweep of road and horizon started to feel like a life you could imagine living.

Memory keeps what cameras miss. On Highway 7 the car carried heat and the sweet edge of pine when a window cracked open; the road wound and climbed, beautiful and relentless, while Kelly’s mother and Ben went carsick—stomach and body out of tune with all that splendor. Voices overlaid the unease: teasing, reassurance, the low murmur of people who were still choosing the same direction. There is humor in that bundle—laughter braided with inconvenience—and strangeness too: how a stretch of road can hurt and still leave you wanting to return. Arkansas became something like home before they lived there, threaded through story before it was address, because love of place rarely arrives only in triumph; sometimes it arrives mixed with nausea and laughter and the stubborn sense that beauty costs something and is still worth it.

She moved mid-semester in the tenth grade—a hard hinge, new lockers while everyone else already knew the shortcuts. She played clarinet in band. On her first day she sat beside a boy who played baritone saxophone, two instruments in the same row the way schools seat strangers until habit turns them familiar. They were only band kids sharing rehearsal and daylight; the chart did not name what three decades would crown. Thirty years later that baritone saxophone player would become her husband, but this chapter is not where love announces itself with certainty. Here they are simply young, adjacent, unknown to their own future.

At Sylvan Hills she fit in well enough that belonging stopped feeling like a prize to re-win each morning. She had friends; she was popular in the plain sense of welcome. Underneath ran journalism—she leaned into it with the appetite of someone who likes sentences more than noise—and she became editor of the school yearbook, deciding what a class would remember and how. Leadership showed early there, quietly, in carrying the year’s story.

In eleventh grade she joined the Student Body Prom Committee while the boy with the baritone served as class president, and the work threw them together again in logistics rather than gossip. The night itself came together at the Statehouse Convention Center as an over-the-top fairy-tale prom—more spectacle than any gym could hold. That evening Kelly attended with the same baritone saxophone player beside her in friendship only. They did not date. They did not kiss. They were friends. Years later that fact would matter more than prom-night gloss ever could, because some stories do not begin where romance begins; they begin earlier, in committees and rehearsals and shared work, unnoticed even by the people inside them.

Faith and family had prepared her voice. Arkansas had become home before she could explain exactly why. School had offered pages and proof that she could lead when leadership meant steady hands. The hearth where she first spoke was small; the rooms ahead would grow wider than anyone in that room could picture. Yet the practice was already honest—not borrowed grandeur. What began as recitations to kin and speeches in hallways would one day need larger air.

For now it was enough that the road had bent toward Arkansas, that two band chairs touched without promising anything, and that some beginnings stay quiet long enough to become true.

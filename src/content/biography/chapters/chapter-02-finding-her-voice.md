# Chapter 2 — Finding Her Voice

Some people hunt for a voice. Others receive one early and spend years learning what it is for. Kelly did not stumble into hers; it was shaped on purpose before classrooms or crowds mattered.

Before microphones there was the living room and the hearth. Her mother would call her forward—stand here, speak clearly, say it again—and what might have been play in another house carried weight in hers: expectation braided with encouragement. She delivered speeches to relatives who already loved her, which meant she owed them clarity and presence anyway. Being taken seriously before the world demands it changes how you see yourself.

At home she had warmth. Through 4-H public speaking she entered rooms where nobody owed her patience—where words had to stand alone. Speaking at home had been practice; outside it became proof that she could hold attention without anyone filling gaps when she faltered. Confidence, she learned early, was less temperament than habit earned by showing up.

Her father’s work with Modern Woodmen of America widened the aperture again: leadership modeled out loud, motivation treated as craft rather than mood. Zig Ziglar loomed large in that atmosphere—not only a name but an idea, that conviction delivered well could shift someone’s direction. The notion settled deep enough that she imagined her own future stage, message, faces tilted toward light.

Years later, past the innocence of those first exposures, she and Steve—her friend long before he was her husband—would ride roads with Zig Ziglar on the tape deck, two people happy to be earnest about the same lessons, laughing at themselves a little for how seriously they took them. It was nerd joy shared in a car: windshields, mileage, and voices on cassette insisting that words still mattered.

Journalism gave that instinct another discipline—not performance for its own sake but attention made coherent. Dixie Martin, her journalism teacher and mentor—among the strongest influences of those school years—had died only recently; the loss sits close when memory turns toward how those stories were learned. Dixie pressed Kelly to see clearly, to organize what others grazed past, and to care whether the truth held on the page. As editor of the school yearbook Kelly wasn’t decorating margins; she was choosing what survived the year—what counted as memory for a whole class. Quiet responsibility like that rarely announces itself as leadership, but it is leadership all the same.

Socially she moved without forcing—listening before insisting, fitting without flattering. Being liked and being leaned on are different things; by then classmates were beginning to rely on her whether they named it or not.

Speaking at home had trained muscle; 4-H had tested it in rooms without cushions; Ziglar had modeled conviction out loud; Dixie had insisted on clarity on the page; friends had begun leaning on her without always naming why—the episodes still looked separate on the surface.

Underneath, habits lined up: plain speech, steady attention to people, stepping forward when the work asked for it. Another thread tugged too, still unnamed—not loud, not theatrical—simply a steadier attention toward children, toward lives easily overlooked. Nothing in those years spelled out where that pull would lead; it only planted patience for a later reckoning.

Ask her then what she would do with her life and she would have answered plainly: she would follow the path those stages had opened—speak, move rooms, change something in the air through words rehearsed until they sounded effortless. Training, repetition, opportunity—all of it seemed to aim one direction.

Lives reorder; rails bend. The voice would matter, and standing in front of people would matter, and discipline and empathy would matter—but not always on the kind of stage she had sketched.

The next step carried her to Lyon College and coursework in psychology—still chasing understanding, still trying to ease hurt with something measurable. On paper it lined up with everything that came before.

Purpose gathered underneath, patient and unfinished, until a moment ahead would ask not only what she could do but what she was willing to carry. She thought she knew the destination. She did not—yet she kept moving, steady enough because confidence had become a practiced skill instead of a lucky trait.

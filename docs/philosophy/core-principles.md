# Core principles

## 1. Common pain → common purpose → common possibility

**Backbone of the movement.**

- **Common pain:** Economic pressure; lack of representation; decisions made *about* people, not *with* them—across rural, urban, and demographic lines.
- **Common purpose:** Fair representation, economic dignity, local control, transparent government.
- **Common possibility:** Demonstrate a system where communities drive decisions, leadership grows locally, and participation matters.

This is **movement architecture**, not only messaging.

## 2. From candidate-centered to community-centered

- Shift from “vote for me, I’ll fix it” to **“let’s build it together—and hold me accountable.”**
- The candidate is a **vehicle**, **connector**, and **translator** between people and policy—not the sole hero. Role: **organizer-in-chief**.

## 3. “Us vs. them” with clarity, not cynicism

- **Us:** everyday Arkansans.  
- **Them:** corporate, political, and financial structures that concentrate benefit and control.

Tone: **clarity and contrast** (who benefits, who decides, who pays)—not anger as the only fuel. Consistent answer: **you’re in the driver’s seat** in what we’re building.

## 4. Grassroots + technology = scalable power

Single **operating system** that combines:

**On the ground**

- Community forums, house parties, faith and school engagement, local leadership pipelines, youth councils.

**In the system (e.g. “Campaign OS” vision)**

- Useful voter/volunteer data (ethics and law compliant), deployment, event→outcome loops, tooling for targeting and messaging, maps of engagement and gaps.

Aspirational loop: **conversation → data → strategy → action.**

## 5. Leadership development as strategy

Build **precinct leaders, community captains, county organizers, youth pipelines**—long-term infrastructure, not disposable volunteers.

Question the work answers: *What if Arkansas had a trained, connected, activated civic network in every community?*

## 6. Radical accessibility and civic education

Disengagement often comes from **confusion, jargon, and closed process**—not apathy.

Commitments:

- Plain-language policy  
- How decisions get made  
- How to engage—not only “please vote”

Path: **participants → advocates → leaders.**

## 7. Accountability as a feature

- Public commitments, feedback loops, measurable outcomes, transparent decisions.  
- Frame: **“Don’t just elect me—track me.”**

## Policy philosophy (pattern, not a full plank list)

**Generally for:** economic opportunity rooted locally; education as empowerment; healthcare access; infrastructure for working families; voting access and fair representation.

**Generally against:** centralized unaccountable power; corporate-over-community bias; participation barriers; opacity.

**Emphasis:** **process and power structure**, not only a laundry list of policies.
